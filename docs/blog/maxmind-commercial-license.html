GeoLite2: Free, But Not "Free"

GeoLite2 is MaxMind's free database tier. It's distributed under the Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike 4.0 (CC BY-SA 4.0) license — and that "ShareAlike" clause is where most developers get tripped up.

What CC BY-SA 4.0 Means in Practice

  1. Attribution: You must give credit to MaxMind wherever the data is used. For a website footer, that's manageable. For an API response or a mobile app, it's awkward at best.
  2. ShareAlike: If you modify or build upon the data, your derivative work must also be licensed under CC BY-SA. This is the real problem — if you embed GeoLite2 in a SaaS product, does your entire product become CC BY-SA? The answer is unclear, and that ambiguity is exactly what lawyers advise against.
  3. No sublicensing: You can't grant your users rights to the GeoLite2 data. If a customer queries your API that uses GeoLite2, they don't have a license to MaxMind's data — and that creates legal exposure.

Bottom line: GeoLite2 is perfect for prototypes, internal tools, and non-commercial projects. Once money is involved — or once a lawyer reviews your stack — the attribution and share-alike requirements become dealbreakers.

GeoIP2: Pay for Certainty

GeoIP2 is MaxMind's commercial database. It has two things GeoLite2 doesn't:

  • 95%+ city accuracy vs ~37% with GeoLite2
  • A clear commercial license that explicitly permits SaaS, API, and redistribution use

The Hidden Cost Nobody Talks About

Even with a GeoIP2 license, you're signing up for operational work:

  • Database downloads: The City database is 50+ GB. You need a server with sufficient disk space, a download script, and a cron job.
  • Update pipeline: Updates arrive weekly (or daily for some editions). You need mmap reload logic so lookups don't fail mid-update.
  • API wrapper: The database is a file. You need to build a REST or gRPC wrapper around it — authentication, rate limiting, monitoring.
  • Multiple environments: Repeat everything for staging, production, and any failover regions.

For a team with dedicated infrastructure engineers, this is a week of work. For an indie developer or small startup, it's a permanent maintenance burden that distracts from building the actual product.

FAQ: MaxMind Licensing Questions Developers Actually Ask

"Can I use GeoLite2 in my SaaS if I add attribution?"

Technically yes, but the ShareAlike clause means your derivative work must also be CC BY-SA licensed. Most SaaS companies are unwilling to open-source their entire product just to use a free IP database. This is why nearly every commercial IP geolocation product uses GeoIP2, not GeoLite2.

"What counts as 'redistribution' of MaxMind data?"

If a third party (your customer, your API user, your app's end user) receives MaxMind data through your product, that's redistribution. Internal analytics dashboards that only your team sees are not redistribution. A public API that returns city-level geolocation to paying customers is.

"Do I need a license if I use a third-party API that uses MaxMind?"

No. If you use an API like IPGeo or ipinfo, the API provider holds the MaxMind license. You're consuming a service, not the database directly. This is exactly why API-based IP geolocation exists — it abstracts away the licensing complexity.

"What happens if I violate MaxMind's license?"

MaxMind's terms allow them to terminate your license and pursue legal remedies. In practice, they typically start with a compliance notice. But if your product depends on IP geolocation, having your data source cut off is an existential risk — one that's not worth taking over a $29/mo API subscription.

MaxMind makes excellent data. Their licensing is reasonable once you understand it, but the barrier to entry — legal review, infrastructure setup, ongoing maintenance — is real.
